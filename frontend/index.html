How Dollar Monitor Works

The Four Inputs

Dollar Monitor tracks four data points from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) system, updated daily:

  • DXY — Trade-Weighted U.S. Dollar Index (DTWEXBGS)
  • 10Y Yield — 10-Year Treasury Constant Maturity Rate
  • Breakeven — 10-Year Breakeven Inflation Rate
  • S&P 500 — S&P 500 Index

Real Yield

Real yield is the 10-year Treasury yield minus the 10-year breakeven inflation rate. It tells you what investors actually earn after expected inflation. Rising real yields attract capital to dollar-denominated assets, strengthening the dollar.

Trend Signals

For DXY and S&P 500, we compute a trend signal as the percentage difference between the 20-day and 100-day moving averages. A positive value means short-term momentum is above the longer-term trend — an uptrend. Negative means downtrend.

USD Score

The USD Score is a weighted composite of three normalized signals:

  • 40% — Real yield (z-score normalized)
  • 30% — DXY trend direction
  • 30% — Inverted S&P 500 trend (stocks down = dollar up)

A score above +0.20 is classified as STRONG USD. Below −0.20 is WEAK USD. In between is NEUTRAL.

Regime Classification

The regime label describes why the dollar is behaving the way it is:

  • RISK-OFF DOLLAR RALLY — Real yields rising while stocks fall. Classic flight-to-safety.
  • YIELD-DRIVEN STRENGTH — High or rising real yields with DXY trending up. Dollar strength from rate differentials.
  • STAGFLATION WATCH — Real yields rising, stocks falling, and inflation expectations rising. The worst-case macro scenario.
  • RISK-ON WEAKNESS — Real yields falling while stocks rise. Capital flowing out of safe havens.
  • NEUTRAL / MIXED — No dominant pattern. Signals conflicting.

Interpretation

The one-sentence interpretation is generated by Claude (Anthropic's AI), which receives all raw and derived data and produces a concise explanation of what is driving the dollar at this moment.

Data sourced from FRED (Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis). Updated on business days, typically with a one-day lag. This is not financial advice.
